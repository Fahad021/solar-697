US Residential Solar Photovoltaic Panel Adoption

A University of Michigan MADS Capstone Project

Collin Brosko, Greg Myers, Robert Underhill, Monica Yen

Introduction

The overall goal of our project is to gain a better understanding of the factors impacting the adoption of photovoltaic solar panels in the residential sector and to provide a means of assessing these factors for different locations within the United States. These factors can be described as ecological, economical, and public policy, or programmatic, features. With the ability to assess trends in solar availability, in the costs and benefits associated with installing and also saving due to solar panels, and to assess the impacts of programs and policies made available, it becomes more possible to understand where there are untapped markets, where adoption may grow, and where programs can kickstart or enhance the rate of adoption.

Irradiance and Meteorological

The Goal

The primary goal of the solar data collection process is to provide quantitative irradiance data for the purpose of estimating photovoltaic electric power generation in a geospatial-context. Secondarily, and of nearly equal importance, is the extraction of trends from the raw data, which can be used to enhance understanding of future trends in solar irradiance and electric power generation potential. Lastly, to incorporate forecasting using one or more models with the goal of providing actionable estimates of future irradiance quantities and to a lesser extent, envisioning future meteorological conditions. Using the irradiance forecast data, estimates of average power generation potential would be available for residential customer photovoltaic investment decision-making. Each of these data product features will be wrapped and presented via the Solar Irradiance Data Explorer dashboard (SIDE-dash).

Background

The central concern with photovoltaic solar power generation is the total available solar irradiance for a given location or region. The absolute availability depends on the calendar season and the latitude of the location. Summertime of the northern hemispheres places the sun higher in the sky and thereby reduces zenith angle or angle of incidence of solar radiation arriving at earth’s upper atmosphere and ultimately the surface of the earth. The decreased zenith angle also reduces the path length through the atmosphere and the corresponding attenuation of available irradiance. As the sun is lower in the wintertime sky, the result is an increase in zenith angle and greater attenuation of available irradiance. An increase in latitude also increases the zenith angle with effects like that of seasonal wintertime.

Meteorological conditions including atmospheric water (Tasie, Banyie, & israel-cookey, 2018) and fine particulate matter also plays a significant role in terrestrial-based photovoltaic electric power production. As light passes through the earth’s atmosphere, varying levels of water vapor (precipitable water) or cloud cover will cause varying levels of diffusion of sun light (wikipedia.com, 2021). Diffusion has the effect of increased zenith as the radiation is no longer straight-line from the sun source, but rather arriving at a higher angle of incidence due to diffusion’s scattering effects. Particulate matter can have a similar effect if not an outright blockage of the sun’s rays. All these factors combine to create a highly seasonal response to solar power production.

The Data

The primary source of solar irradiance data is obtained from the National Renewable Energy Laboratory (NREL, 2021) and the National Solar Radiation Database (NSRDB). NREL is an organization of the U.S. Department of Energy and a globally respected source of renewable energy data, recommended by industry and academia sources alike. More specifically, the on-demand data was requested through the NREL provided API which allows client-specific location, timeframe, and data feature requests. The NSRDB, provided by NREL, is a 30-minute time increment, 4-km spatially gridded dataset with complete coverage of North America, and more. For each of the ZIP Code-based locations requested, all data fields and all twenty-three years of 1-hour increment data (1998-2020) were downloaded. The full data set include the following fields:

●       date_time, zipcode, location_id, Year, Month, Day, Hour

●       Clearsky_DHI, DHI, Clearsky_DNI, DNI, Clearsky_GHI, GHI

●       Temperature, Dew_Point, Relative_Humidity, Precipitable_Water

●       Pressure, Wind_Direction, Wind_Speed, Cloud_Type, Fill_Flag

●       Solar_Zenith_Angle, Surface_Albedo

●       Global_Horizontal_UV_Irradiance_(280-400nm)

●       Global_Horizontal_UV_Irradiance_(295-385nm)]

 

 

Each of these fields is retained in the project’s Zip Code request databases, but not in the current monthly aggregated database. This is mostly due to file size restrictions imposed by GitHub. While not a part of the project GitHub repository, all requested raw data downloads have been retained for future use as necessary.

The current ZIP Code spatial data was extracted from the Gazetteer Files page of U.S. Census Bureau (census.gov, 2021). The Gazetteer data includes a geo-spatially centered latitude/longitude coordinate for each ZIP Code polygon. The ZIP Code geo-coordinate was used as the location parameter in the NREL API queries of the NSRDB dataset. Additional fields in the Gazetteer data include land and water area for each ZIP Code. This data could have future use in determining if additional NSRDB coverage is necessary given the spatial extents of a given ZIP Code.

A third and final data source is UnitedStatesZipCodes.org which has available an up-to-date dataset (UnitedStatesZipCodes.org, 2021) that relates ZIP Codes with geo-political features such as city, county, state, and more. This dataset along with the Gazetteer data formed the basis for the ‘geo_zipcodes’ table used in the project database(s).

The Approach

As a matter of portability and resource minimization, SQLite3 was determined to be the database of choice for the project. SQLite3 is a part of the Python Standard Library and functional on all compute platforms which support Python.

The ‘geo_zipcodes’ database was an early creation to be used as feedstock in the production of the actual irradiance database. Certain fields are updated within the ‘geo_zipcodes’ database with each download of new irradiance data. When a new irradiance database is built, the current ‘geo_zipcodes’ database is incorporated as the ‘geo_zipocodes’ table.

When a Zip Codes-based request for irradiance data is made from a team member, the ‘get_zipcode_geos.ipynb’ notebook script compiles the ZIP Codes into a simple YAML text file that is ingested by the ‘nsrdb_download.ipynb’ notebook script. As each NSRDB request arrives, minor data manipulations are performed, and the data is then written to the new ZIP Code irradiance database. Simultaneously the internal ‘geo_zipcodes’ table is updated as is the external database for future use.

Because all facets of the project operate at the monthly level, the choice to aggregate working data at the monthly level was an easy choice. Originally this operation was performed in real-time once the data was ingested from the database. As SIDE-dash took shape, irradiance data ingestion performance failed to meet expectations as twenty-three years of hourly data resulted in nearly 201,500 records.

As a late addition to the project, the ‘nsrdb_aggregator.ipynb’ notebook script was authored to reduce the overall size of the dataset while still retaining current functionality. The reduction in data volume was profound; a 43 ZIP Code irradiance database of raw data consumed 1.1GB of filesystem space. The aggregated hourly average by month database, of the same ZIP Codes, was reduced to 5.9MB.

An example of hourly average by month; ZIP Code 13214 (Syracuse, New York), for the month of January 1998, has as Global Horizontal Irradiance (GHI) value of 56.9 Wh/m^2 (Watt hours per square meter); meaning that on average, for every hour of the 24-hour day, 56.9 Wh/m^2 of solar radiation arrived at the earth’s surface in Syracuse. As a point of reference Phoenix, Arizona received, on average, 144.8 Wh/m^2 per hour for the same month. Obviously, the sun does not shine 24-hours a day in these regions therefore the total irradiance (1,365.6 and 3475.2 respectively) would be captured during normal daylight hours for the location in question.

The dashboard currently includes two forecasting models built from the PMDARIMA open-source time-series package. The first model performs a Fast Fourier Transform (FFT) of the data to eliminate the seasonal component of the data; a necessary and important step for any time-series forecast model. A simple model pipeline is implemented to ensure that the transform is correctly applied to all data, be it training, validation or test. The second model relies on more traditional seasonal decomposition (parameters P, D, Q) to eliminate seasonality from the data. Both models incorporate standard Order parameters p, d, and q for auto-regressive (AR), differencing (Integration), and moving average (MA). The benefit of the PMDARIMA package is the automatic Order optimization methods that are built into the library. In the context of SIDE-dash, each forecast is optimized for the specific feature selected. An interesting note is the FFT model is typically three to five times faster than the traditional seasonal decomposition model. The metric Root Mean Squared Error (RMSE) was chosen as the model test performance indicator and comparator. The advantage of RMSE is that the calculated value retains the original units of the training and forecast data, simplifying its interpretation and comparison.

The Outcome

The primary goal of the irradiance data collection process is the production of a database(s) product that all team members can easily query and retrieve irradiance data for application to their respective needs. The outcome of this goal is realized through the Solar Irradiance Data Explorer dashboard that provides multiple exploratory views of the aggregated data, decomposed trend data, and time-series forecasting of the data. While the irradiance data and forecast would see the most direct use, the trend data offers a tantalizing perspective of future potential.

Economic

The Goal

In the Sixth Assessment Report published by The Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change, the scientists have made it abundantly clear that the time is now to make drastic changes to help curb climate change before it is too late. Solar technology has been around since the mid-17th century and has been well researched. Albert Einstein even won a Nobel Prize for his theories on the photovoltaic effect in 1921. Even with technology that is over 150 years old, it is not a secret that the solar panels for residential use are expensive. Every year the solar technology gets better, and the residential consumer is presented with more options for clean and renewable energy; but there are still obstacles that hinder adoption.  Primarily for residential consumers, there are many costs that go into a decision regarding the adoption of solar panels in a region and how beneficial they will be to a community. There is more that goes into the economic considerations of solar panel adoption than the overall purchasing and installation cost of the units. The economics of solar panel adaptation for residential users considers the cost of energy provided by the utility companies, as well as the total energy consumed by a community. We aim to examine multiple aspects of solar panel adoption at the residential level to define those states that might have the highest return on investment and states that will yield the greatest positive impact if solar technology is utilized. The economics of solar panels are only one element to consider when evaluating the investment, but many customers view the cost of installation as the primary obstacle, “Costs for solar can sometimes be overwhelming, or make people think that it’s not possible for them” (Marcilla, M, 2021). In addition to the cost considerations, the public policy analysis and climatological study will help to identify which geographic areas in the US will benefit from the transition to solar technology.  

The Background

One of the main concerns for potential residential solar customers is the initial financial cost. The initial installation cost for solar panels can be significant but prospective customers must be aware that solar technology should be thought of as a multi-year investment. It is because of this that forecasted costs are important for this evaluation.

The Data

The cost impact of solar panels for residential customers really comes down to two aspects: first being the initial cost of purchase and installation, and second being the cost avoidance from the utility companies. While solar panel technology has been around for a long time, it has only recently been used at the individual consumer level. Therefore, the data is limited on quantities of installation and the total cost of installation on a large scale. There is a U.S. Department of Energy National Laboratory Managed by the University of California known as Berkeley Lab that has been compiling and analyzing solar installation data for years and they have published their findings. The Berkeley Lab was able to compile a substantial database of information including installation date, size of installation, total installation price, and assorted geographic markers. All this data goes back to 1998 and one of the most comprehensive databases for solar installation projects out there. The only obstacle that existed with this database was the depth of data. There were many states that reported zero residential solar installations and other states that had a minimal amount of data available to accurately calculate a trend, so certain limitations existed.

The U.S Energy Information Administration published yearly energy cost per kilowatt-hour from 1990 to present at the state level. This data will help us create a benchmark and forecast energy cost out through 2030. The same governing body also published the total energy sold at the state level for the same time based on total megawatt-hours. These values will give us the ability to forecast which states are projected to consume the most amount of energy delivered from the utility companies for the next 10 years.  The U.S Energy Information Administration data proved to be an excellent source for total energy cost and total state energy usage dating back to 1990. The only way that the data could have been improved was if the information was able to be compiled at a zip-code or county level.

The Approach

Since the adoption of solar panels as a source of energy is a multifaceted decision for any residential customer, certain rated scores were created. The overall installation cost of solar panels was given a weighted score of 30% due to the initial investment. The next 20% of our weighted score came from the total energy usage at the state level. As the energy grid in the US becomes larger and more unstable due to several environmental factors and due to the age of the grid, high energy usage can be a driver for more consumers to become less reliant on utility companies. Frequent blackouts and unreliable service can be alleviated by an investment in renewable energy. The final and largest proportion of our weighted score comes from the cost of energy. “It is projected that the residential electricity price in the United States will increase by 1.3 percent between 2021 and 2022. Generally, retail electricity prices in the country have risen over the last decade” (EIA, 2021). As energy costs continue to rise, consumers will begin to look for alternative solutions and solar technology is the most logical solution for many consumers. Switching from being reliant on a utility company for energy to being self-reliant or even partially self-reliant will be a decision that the residential consumer will have to make for themselves.

As energy costs continue to rise, consumers will begin to look for alternative solutions and solar technology is the most logical solution for many consumers. Switching from being reliant on a utility company for energy to being self-reliant or even partially self-reliant will be a decision that the residential consumer will have to make for themselves. Considering the previous years’ worth of data and our designated weighting scores for each criterion, a ten-year forecast was created using an autoregressive moving average model. Since an investment in solar panels should not be thought of as a short-term solution, we make the decision to rank each state at their forecasted values in 2030 to calculate our high-yield states for solar panel adoption.

  

The Outcome

States were given a weight score based on installation cost, total energy consumption, and cost of energy and forecasted these values out for the next ten years to accommodate the long-term economic impact of solar panel usage at the residential level. Based on these weight scores, we can identify the top 10 years in which the adoption and use of solar technology could have the most beneficial economic impact on residential consumers.

While some of these states are not surprising to see on our top 10 list, other states are surprising. Consider that this suggestion is only due to economic factors that are weighted most heavily on the cost of energy.

It is important to consider that many of the utility companies are also investing in renewable technology to drive down the cost of energy for its consumers and this factor was not considered in this analysis. Additionally, data on solar panel installation was limited but it is likely that the availability of this data will continue to improve as the technology becomes more widely available and utilized.

Future Work

Economic drivers may include household income and states' current investment in alternative renewable energy. Policy and Programmatic

The Goal

 

In the advent of new technologies, it is often programs and policies that can kickstart adoption as a means of making a new technology more attractive, most often through means of driving costs down or improving payback periods. Throughout the period in which solar panels have been offered to residential customers, there have been a wide array of programs and policies offered. The goal of this segment of the project is to assess the effectiveness of these programs, primarily by type.

 

The Data  

 

 

Berkeley Lab maintains data for over 80% of all solar installations in the United States through their “Tracking the Sun” report. This information is reported through several channels and as such, some is only maintained at the state level. Some states do not have reported data. The above chart contains markers for each zip code in which Berkeley Lab has data to that level. This data consists of cost, system size, recorded rebate information, the date of installation and several other features for each installation recorded.

 

NREL maintains a trove of irradiance data. For this portion of the project, the NREL_PSM_2019 measure was utilized. This is maintained as a TIF image file which can be mapped to zip codes. Future project iterations may integrate output from the irradiance and meteorological section should all relevant zip codes be available.

 

Policies have been tracked by the North Carolina Clean Energy Technology Center and organized into a dataset known as the Database of State Incentives for Renewables & Efficiency. This data indicates program types, start dates, specific features and provides connections from the programs to affected zip codes.

 

The US Energy Information Administration (EIA) captures the cost of electricity by sector and by state dating back to January of 2001. While this does not give a specific zip code level and does not date back to the first installation in the Berkeley dataset (January, 1998), the variability of initial installations along with the scarcity of early installations, along with the perceived significance of the cost of electricity in adoption, led to the decision to use this data and cut off installation data prior to 2001. The data was mapped to month and zip code.

 

Housing data was obtained from the 2019 American Community Survey and 2010 census. A linear connection was mapped to years between. Without earlier dates identified, housing numbers were maintained at constant 2010 values for earlier years, though this would ideally be updated for future iterations.

The Approach

 

The project began with informational interviews with the City of Ann Arbor and with the North Carolina Clean Energy Technology Center. These interviews laid out the landscape for what is residential solar adoption and the factors that might impact it. Solar adoption, while gaining steam, is still relatively in its infancy with some parts of the county moving at much higher rates than others. Factors of available solar energy, costs of electricity and cost of installation were treated as the primary factors in adoption. Program types were evaluated versus these primary factors in determining the effect of their implementations.

 

Policy and program information, provided in CSV form needed to be reassembled into a database form. Once assembled, further manipulation was needed in order to map all information to the zip code level, whether original programs we provided at state, utility or other level initially. The data was filtered to include only policies which impacted the residential sector, not including multi-family homes. The decision to not include multi-family homes is due to the belief that the decision-making process for a single-family homeowner would be different from that of a multifamily residence property manager/ owner. This further allowed for decisions to be made regarding which installations were to be considered residential within the Berkeley Lab dataset. The program type, start and end dates, along with impacted zip codes were organized into a dictionary which was then used to map program types to the zip codes and dates in which they were believed to be effective. For rebate and grant programs, the effective dates were assumed based upon correlation to grant and rebate values within the Berkeley dataset. Other programs were assessed at a 12-month window as well as assuming no end date.

 

Information utilized for this project from the Berkeley data set included the zip code, date, size, cost and rebates pertaining to each installation. Installations were filtered by what Berkeley Lab listed as residential. Additionally, ground mounted installations were filtered out due to associated costs, though this may be a considered revision for future work. Installations with battery storage were also filtered due to the costs included not specific to the solar generation capability. Cost outliers were determined through a principal component analysis of date versus cost. This trimmed a small number of data points. The final filter was to limit installed generation capacity to 25 kW. This is viewed as a reasonable limit for a single-family home and is also a cap value for some programs.

 

The National Renewable Energy Lab maintains a measure of irradiance known as the “nsrdb_full_disc_2019_dni_scaled.” This data was mapped to and averaged upon the zip code boundaries provided. It was then merged with other data.

 

Installation counts were normalized against housing counts to provide a more comparable metric across zip codes that may be more or less dense.

 

Initial reviews of data and regressions showed that of the potential non-policy features, the Irradiance measure, price of electricity and cost of installation were by far more significant than other obtained features, with cost of installation being the least significant of the three. These values were treated as constants within approaches to causal inference.

 

A statsmodels OLS regression was performed along with a program-by-program Causal Model with propensity matching. This provided a general understanding of the effectiveness of program types. Upon reviewing program data in greater detail, some programs may vary in their levels of implementation and as such, some programs within a given type may skew a type to become more or less effective.

 

Due to this potential issue, a separate analysis platform was developed to evaluate individual programs. To do this, program types may be filtered to the individual program. The relevant zip codes are then identified and matched based upon the primary features identified previously along with the general installation trend prior to implementation. Done at the monthly level for processing capability, the implementation month itself is treated as a center point as programs may have been implemented in the first or final day of a month. All pairs are aggregated making up a control and treatment group. The six-month prior to six-month following period is then plotted for comparison. This will allow for individual program level evaluation of treatment effect.

 

The Outcome

 

In general, feedback obtained through interviews was confirmed through the data. This stated that the driving forces behind solar adoption have been available solar energy, cost of energy and programs which offer longer-term benefits.

 

While definitive treatment effects for program types cannot be determined, the current results show that certain program types have been generally more effective than others. Tax-oriented programs, whether personal tax credits, personal tax deductions or property tax incentives are generally shown to be effective. Additionally, programs which offer long-term payback potential, such as feed-in tariffs or value of solar tariffs, are also shown to be effective. Perhaps unexpectedly, instant return / discount type programs such as rebates or grants were not shown to be effective in increasing residential solar adoption. Other programs had limited instances and could not be evaluated with as much confidence.

As indicated earlier, programs may vary within a given type. As such, nuance may be evaluated by looking at individual programs. In many cases, this provides a clearer impact of a program.
